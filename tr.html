Price Action

Welcome to the Trading Blueprint. First, I would like to thank you for purchasing this guide. 

In the following pages, I believe that I will be able to shed some light on trading, which should eventually improve you as a trader.

Before I start to cover the trading strategy I use, I would like to say a few things about trading as a whole and my general experience and thoughts.

In the last couple of years, I spend a lot of time trading or doing things connected to trading and financial markets. 

Was it worth it?

I ask myself pretty frequently as the actual trading is the straight opposite of what you see on social media.

There are very few industries that have a worse image than trading.

This is thanks to people who portray trading as something you can easily do from your phone sitting at the beach.

What is trading all about? 

Although there is still a chance that I didn’t crack a “secret Forex iPhone millionaire” code, it is pretty safe to say that the real trading experience is very different from how it is presented online.

I never really counted how many hours I have spent learning or just staring at the charts, but as I have been sitting around 10-12 hours in front of a computer with at least one screen related to trading, it will be a lot.

Moreover, the market is random. There is a great 4-part series by Mark Douglas about it on Youtube as well.

The strategy I will show you in this guide has a very logical approach and (at least for me) makes a lot of sense, but that doesn’t change the fact that every trade is just a coin flip.

The edge comes from a long-term positive expectancy, but the outcome of every single trade is purely random.

This is why there will be trades that you categorize as an A+ opportunity, which will go straight to the dumpster the minute you enter.

And there are also going to be trades that you will doubt a lot or skip entirely, yet they will play out perfectly.

Thanks to this randomness and added the fact that you will spend the majority of your day watching charts.

Trading can be pretty exhausting as losing days/weeks and sometimes even months can happen.

So, why trade?

Everyone has different reasons; for most of us, they involve money, freedom, travelling, etc.

If you didn’t get it by now, there is not much freedom and travelling involved.

At least if you decide to focus on intraday/intraweek trading, it is your best chance to make it anyway.

 I will talk about this a little later on.

But with all this being said, trading is a unique and rewarding thing to do.

Although it becomes boring after some time, which is good, I enjoy coming to the market every day and looking for opportunities, patterns and watching the behaviour and intention of different participants in the market.

The goal of this introduction wasn’t to turn you off.

But everyone who wants to make it in trading should be prepared to sacrifice a lot for it and, most notably, if everyone online is telling or showing you something too good to be true.

Don’t trust them

One last thing that is worth mentioning is that there is no superior strategy. You can have someone who trades moving average crossovers or harmonic patterns to outperform someone with any complex and advance trading strategy.

As there is no right or wrong in the market, trading is all about finding what works for you and what suits you.

I already said this strategy I am about to show you have a very logical approach and works for me, but it is not sure that it will work for you.

The market as an advertising mechanism

The only purpose market has to advertise prices for buyers and sellers.

It facilitates trades through what is known as the dual auction process.

One of the things you might hear quite often when the market sharply moves to one direction is that there were more buyers than sellers in the market.

This is not true since, for every buyer, there has to be a seller and vice versa.

When the price goes up or down, there is no supremacy on either side, but the amount of aggression or willingness to buy/sell for higher/lower prices.

In other words, the market is always seeking fair value based on supply and demand dynamics. 

This doesn’t count only in trading financial markets, but the same mechanism of auction works at every market in the world.

This whole process is called Auction Market Theory, which I will cover later on.

 

Something that probably never gets old in the trading industry is the whole idea of “Market maker running retail traders stop losses and manipulating the whole market.” 

If you never went to this rabbit hole, type “Market Maker” or “forex stop hunting” on YouTube, and you will have quite a lot of crazy content to digest.

When I was looking at this myself recently, someone called his YouTube video, “This is How Market Makers STEAL YOUR MONEY!” and put a picture of George Soros in the thumbnail.

This is how crazy these people are. 

Their sole purpose is to sell you something as concepts for market manipulation, and beating the large players are indeed good marketing terms.

Although I’m not the biggest fan of Macrohedged and Macrodesiac, this video gives a great explanation of market-making so if you want to learn more about the topic, give it a listen.

I don’t know your trading account size, but I’m quite sure that you are not sitting on multi-million dollar fund money; therefore, your orders have zero impact on the market movements.

Thus, retail traders, both you and I cannot do much about the market movements.

Markets are moved by institutional traders, banks, funds, and whatnot.

As I already mentioned, the popular opinion is that markets are constantly manipulated, and every large institution is hunting the retail traders’ stop-losses.

I will let you be the judge if you believe it or not, as to understand my strategy, and this guide is not important at all.

On the other hand, what is essential is that institutional traders have a disadvantage compared to retail traders.

They cannot enter the market anywhere they want because they would experience significant slippage.

That is why they have to be matched with enough liquidity to execute their orders.

So why your stop-loss always gets hit before the move you have anticipated play out?

As everyone tends to put their stop-losses or other resting orders at undeniable places, the market likes to revisit those before any significant moves. There is enough liquidity to fill large order sizes. I will talk about this more in-depth later on.

 

Above, you can have a look at this heatmap chart.

As you can see, the most significant amount of orders is always resting below support and above resistance, which is represented here with the yellow lines.

There is no secret market manipulation voodoo but basic human behaviour.

The only thing that market is doing is seeking fair value.

It moves from areas of balance, where buyers and sellers agree on prices, 

to an imbalance where aggressive buyers overcome sellers and vice versa.

These moves are caused by supply and demand imbalances. 

When demand exceeds, the supply market tends to move up.

When the supply exceeds the demand, the market moves down.

Learning how to trade both balanced and imbalanced markets and spotting genuine breakouts of the balance is one of the essential skills every successful trader should have.

Market Structure

Market Structure is a fundamental topic that everyone learns somewhere at the beginning of their trading journey.

Yet so many people can get it wrong.

The image at the right shows the textbook example of a trending market.

Higher highs and higher lows represent the uptrend.

When the last higher low, which made the highest high, break, and the market makes a lower low, we can assume that trend was broken, and the market structure shifted.

After that, the downtrend is represented by lower highs and lower lows.

The trend is relative to the timeframe you are trading as we can have a trend (imbalance) on a lower timeframe but range (balance) on a higher timeframe for the same instrument.

Are things simple like this in actual trading? Of course not.

If you look at the image that shows the actual chart, you can see that things can get rather messy.

Although the market structure is not always clean like the chart on the left, I often use the textbook behaviour assumption.

One of the most crucial things is the trend changes.

This is where several of my price action patterns come to play.

Being able to spot a trend change on the lower timeframe in the higher timeframe direction is one of the best ways of growing any trading account.

The uptrend is broken once we have a candle close below the swing low that made the highest high on the time frame we are trading.

The downtrend is broken once we have a candle close above the swing high that made the lowest low on the time frame we are trading.

I know that you probably didn’t sign up for something as simple as this, but understanding market structure changes have to become second nature before we can move forward

Orderflow and Liquidity

If you open your broker’s trade window, you will always see two different prices for your instrument.

They are called the bid/ask.

The ask (also called offer) shows the lowest offer price which the market is willing to sell at and for you to buy.

The bid shows the highest bid price which the market is ready to buy at and for you to sell.

There are two types of orders, “market orders” and “limit orders”. 

On the right side, you can see an example of the Trading DOM, which shows bid and ask at different price levels.

At the 172.14 level, 466 lots are sitting at the offer.

It can be one seller advertising 466 lots, or it can be 466 sellers advertising one lot.

It doesn’t matter. What matters is that it has to trade through these 466 lots first for the price to move. 

As I already mentioned, there are two types of orders, market and limit orders.

The limit orders are called passive order flow, also known as liquidity.

Limit orders are often the heavier hand in the market as larger players typically use them to prevent slippage. If you are using a limit order, you are a liquidity maker as you put liquidity to the order book.

If you execute the market order, you are a liquidity taker as you take liquidity from the order book.

Everyone can place a limit order into the order book and advertise the prices for which he/she is willing to buy/sell. But until the order is executed, It does not mean anything. 

Once the order is executed, it becomes a part of the active order flow.

Stop losses and stop orders that serve to get trader in or out of the position play a vital role. 

They are considered limit orders, and when they are executed, it adds pressure in the opposite direction.

In other words, when you are long, and the market goes against you, you are going to use a sell stop order to get you out of the position, which someone can use as liquidity to fill his long at the lower price.

Big market participants are moving the markets by consuming orders. 

Their orders generate buying/selling pressure (momentum). Pushing the price in its direction, consuming smaller orders until it stops (slows down).

Because they have a hard time getting filled on their positions all at once, they stack their orders at the levels where they moved the market in the past. 

This is the called order-stacking. Orders are stacked at the areas of supply and demand imbalances.

For significant market participants to move the markets, they need enough liquidity to do that.

The order flow and market microstructure are complex topics. If you want to learn more about it, here are some additional resources.

Financial Market Microstructure Lecture by Egor Starkov

Why is order flow so persistent? By Bence Tóth and others

How markets slowly digest changes in supply and demand by Jean-Philippe Bouchaud

The Significance of Timerame – Great video by Young Tilopa

If we talk about liquidity, we are talking a passive order flow, also known as resting orders.

As we know, for every buyer, there has to be a seller and vice versa.

This is considered a stable state of the market as it creates a balance between supply and demand.

When we have an imbalance in the supply and demand dynamics, any given instrument’s value starts to change.

The balanced state on a chart is represented by consolidation in price, and a high volume/momentum candle presents the imbalance.

Once you see a high momentum candle in the market, we are entering a low liquidity state. 

These happen due to order flow imbalances once buyers overcome sellers (vice versa) and market moves.

These liquidity gaps tend to get filled, and because they were used as a point of origin for big traders to accumulate their positions, they are often reused once the price comes back. 

In context, this can be a great area to enter the position.

All you have to do is seek high momentum candles. These are where liquidity gaps / low volume nodes are created.

The position is then entered upon filling the liquidity gap.

Without context, these are mediocre trades at best, but you can easily spot those high momentum candles and build a trading idea around it

.

 

If you go back a little bit, you can have a look at the heatmap chart posted.

With the resting orders below support and above resistance comes the colossal topic of liquidity pools.

Not as huge as necessary, but huge as it is used by everyone to sell you the idea of evil market makers trying to hunt your stop-loss.

As I already mentioned, this is not true, but the areas with resting liquidity are essential nonetheless.

So what are liquidity pools? These are just areas where the market is going to meet a lot of resting orders.

We can find these areas above/below daily or weekly highs/lows, double tops or bottoms, general s/r levels, etc.

As these areas bring a ton of attention, you will have many resting orders there in the form of stop losses, but also stop market orders from traders anticipating a breakout.

Opposite of this are people who took SFP’s concept and started to trade every single false break at swing highs and swing lows.

If you decide to trade every false break above the level, as I often see people do on Twitter without any other context.

You will end up with inferior results.

This is why I never trade these failed swings blindly with only information provided from a candlestick chart.

If I can use a footprint chart where I have a detailed look at what happened inside the bar.

How I trade Support and Resistance

Before I show you the actual setups, there are two more theoretical concepts to cover.

The first one is support and resistance.

The prevalent misconception is that with more touches, S/R level “firm up” and are more robust. 

Truth is the opposite as the orders resting at S/R levels get consumed with multiple touches. The aggressive participants are more likely to break the level once they don’t have to deal with a huge amount of passive orderflow.

My biggest problem with simple horizontal support and resistance is that things are very rarely simple in trading.

Therefore you will very rarely get this perfect support that flip and turn resistance.

That being said, there are two ways how to trade support and resistance.

From the same side (support tested as support/resistance tested as resistance) or on a flip side (support become resistance/resistance become support).

When a price is testing a level from the same side, I never look for blind entry. 

Why? Because of the liquidity pools that are often below/above the levels.

One of the worst things that can happen is to enter the trade to see it hitting our stop loss before going our direction. 

That is likely to happen once you trade a level from the same side.

When you are trading a level from the same side, you always anticipate the price visiting a liquidity pool. Because of that, you never set up a blind limit order at the level.

Limit orders can be set once levels flip and you trade the flip side of the level.

As we all know, broken support becomes resistance and broken resistance becomes support.

In order for the level to flip there has to be a significant imbalance between buyers and sellers that cause levels to probe.

These levels offer a great opportunity especially when we put them together with supply and demand dynamics.

My approach to support and resistance is a bit different so that I do not mark out levels using price action but rather use other tools that I will describe later on.

These levels I use as my higher time frame map, and then I look for patterns and other factors of confluence to my entry.

I don’t pay too much attention to how the levels line up with candlesticks on the chart, but rather look for acceptance/rejection at high volume nodes’ edges.

This will make much more sense a little later on.

If you decide to stick with mostly price action, there are only two rules.

If you are trading the level from the same side, always wait for the market to trade at the last swing high or swing low. If you see a rejection with a wick closing above the low or below the previous candle high, it is “swept”.

Trading the opposite (support turned resistance and vice versa), I use FTR that I explain later on. But you can place a limit order at the list high/or low before the breakout.

 

Supply and Demand Imbalances

Every market is being moved by supply and demand dynamics.

Buyers represent the demand, and sellers represent the supply.

Thanks to the ongoing auction between those two parties, the market is moving.

When they agree on prices, markets tend to be balanced (ranging).

But when there is more demand than supply, the price of any given product will rise as demand exceeds supply.

When there is more supply than demand, any given product’s price will fall as supply exceeds demand.

As I already mentioned, in the areas where supply exceeds demand and vice versa, we can find stacked orders.

These stacked unfilled orders then provide a reaction once the market returns to the level.

 

In the previous chapter, I showed you a screenshot with liquidity gaps and explained how price enters the low liquidity state due to an imbalance in the order books.

If the market strongly moves to the upside, we can say that demand overcame supply. At the origin of that move, we can find the demand zone.

Demand Zone – Down candles before an illiquid move up. The move has to be significant enough to break the market structure. (Closing above the previous swing high.)

The opposite is also true when the market makes a strong move to the downside; supply overcame demand.

At the origin of that move, we can find our supply zone.

Supply Zone – Up candles before an illiquid move down. The move has to be significant enough to break the market structure. (Closing below the previous swing low.)

 

You can add many other factors, such as how strong the market left the level, how it came back, how far it went before it went back, how many times the level was tested, and so on.

As I am not a supply and demand trader, I like to keep things relatively simple, so I follow only two rules.

First, I only trade the level on the first time back; on second or third touch, I look for an SFP to happen, and I am not bothered by supply/demand.

The second is that I never trade V-shape reversal to the level.

Trading the FTR – Failure to return

I have learned a lot of things I know about price action from different threads in the forex factory.

Back in the day, there was someone who runs the thread called RTM.

I wont to go the details of the cult following he built around him and the fact he stole a lot of his stuff, which he started selling for 15,000 pounds later on.

But as he focused on supply and demand trading, I learned the term FTR there for the first time.

Over the years, I adjusted it to my liking, but as I never change the names of concepts when I learn them, the name FTR stuck with me.

So why “Failure to Return”? Once the price hits key support or resistance area, you expect it will return and go the opposite side.

For example, a market that is in a downtrend and starts a rally to the resistance area is more likely to return to the downside once it hits this resistance level.

 

Failure to return happens when the market starts to pull back at the resistance, making a move down first to create a demand zone before breaking the resistance to the upside.

FTR is created when the market starts turning before the key support/resistance area to lure buyers/sellers in. These buyers/sellers are used as liquidity by larger players to push the market to the opposite side and breakout from the key area.

Once the breakout is complete, plenty of traders are trapped, which we often see with a high negative/positive delta (explained later).

When the market retest those levels, these offside positions use the opportunity to get out for break-even/slight loss that adds fuel to the move.

 

Trading the O/U – Over and Under

I believe that Paracurve first introduced the Quasimodo/Over and Under setup in 2009.

Since then, it becomes a trendy price action setup used by a lot of people.

It is probably the one I have been using the most in my trading. The fact that it can be used in both trending and mean-reverting environments make it pretty unique.

The simplest explanation would be that O/U pattern is very similar to the head and shoulders pattern, but instead waiting for the break of a neckline, we will be trading the right shoulder.

Over/Under occurs when the market breaks out the support/resistance area but cannot sustain the momentum and falls back in a manner strong enough that causes a break in the market structure.

When we are trading O/U pattern, we are looking for entry at prior support/resistance level before it was broken (also known as the left shoulder).

Your stop loss goes above/below the high/low(the head). And our target is a new high/low.

Later on, I will show you how I use it with order flow confluence, but I tend to use O/U a lot as my mean-reverting swing setup.

Trading the V-shape

This is not a pattern per se but rather a specific market behavior that happens quite often.

With three price action setups I trade, I use limit order in two of them, and even with SFP, I usually watch for them on higher timeframes and then look for either FTR or O/U on LTF where I once again place my limit.

There is only one situation in my trading where I use a market order, which I will show you later on.

Anyway, the point is that my trading revolves a lot around waiting for the market to show me signs of strength, and then I get in on pullback.

What I have found out is that not only the impulse move is essential, but also if the market accepts new prices or not.

I start canceling my orders when the market got back “too-fast” to my entry line.

There are no exact rules for what I consider “too-fast,” but usually, when the market is back to my entry after breakout within 1-3 candles for a given time-frame I trade, I pull the order out.

If the price finishes the V-shape reversal, it provides the opportunity to trade its retest.

It usually looks something like this.

As I already mentioned,  what would be enough space or too-fast pullback is very subjective.

This is why I tend to get stopped out often because I leave my limit orders when I feel retest had enough acceptance. If you start using Market Profile, 2 TPOs usually signal the acceptance, but it doesn’t work all the time.

Because of that, what I find more important about V-Shape is not to be safe 100% times with pulled order, but rather the opportunity to get into the opposite direction once proven wrong.

Problem with swing trading

Do you know what you are going to do in the next 10 minutes?

I am sure you do.

How about the next 1-hour, 10-hours, 1 or 3 days?

I think you have a decent idea about all of these, but you are less and less confident with additional time as things can change.

For example, if I have planned a vacation for tomorrow, there is a decent chance that I will wake up tomorrow morning and go on vacation, but if my holiday is planned in 2 months, quite a lot of things can happen.

Considering what happened this year with the pandemic situation, you sure know how rapidly things can change.

Auction Market Theory

The Auction Market Theory breaks down the market’s primary purpose and how its participants interact to fulfill this purpose.

Trading is no different from any other auctions where there are only two main goals to be achieved.

  • Facilitate trade and act as a place where buyers and sellers meet
  • Seek for fair value

When prices are too high/low, and below/above fair value, the buyers/sellers are expected to push prices back to the fair value; this is called responsive activity.

Then market trades above/below fair value, buying/selling is not expected, it is called initiating activity, which is when we can see markets to trend until it finds new fair value.

As in every auction, volume confirms or rejects new prices.

 

Stock prices are a straightforward example.

Let’s say that right now, Samsung stock is trading at $50.

A New Samsung phone comes out, and it’s bad; the battery is not working, overheating, etc.

Because of this event, Samsung stock starts to dropping on its value until it finds new buyers at let’s say $30 per share price.

This is where is the new value area created.

After some time, phones get repaired, and the price of the stock starts to rise again. Where is the market likely to stop? Previous value area around $50.

This is eventually what every market does as Market participants negotiate prices between balanced and imbalanced values.

Auction Market Theory defines an area where 68% of the volume has traded as a Value Area.

 

There are 3 key components of Auction Market Theory:

  • Price – Advertise opportunity in the market
  • Time – Regulate price opportunity
  • Volume – Measure the success of failure of the auction. Volume is variable and represents the interaction of market participants at different levels.

To sum the Auction Theory up you always have to understand the context of a given market as there are two types of auction.

Trending market – When market is trending up/down we can see a fair value constantly moving in the direction of the trend.

Ranging market – Balance between buyers and sellers that is represented by overlapping values.

The general rule of thumb is once the market is inside the value it will more likely stay in balance and explore inside the range of the value.

But if the market is an imbalance, it will often drift higher/lower until it stops which is usually inside a previous value area.

Balance

  • Bell shaped distribution 
  • Symmetrical
  • Buyers and sellers agree on prices
  • Liquidity resting above and below the balance

Failed Auction 

  • When price comes outside of the value and does not gain acceptance
  • No increase in volume on breakout
  • No changes in delta
  • Obvious rejection – I often look for SFP or O/U on price chart

Acceptance

  • Large increase in volume
  • Strong price action through the area
  • Major change in delta
  • Price are accepted with time and space
  • FTR or V-Shape are often seen as breakout patterns

Market Profile

Market Profile, also known as TPO (time-price-opportunity), arranges separate trading sessions to the so-called profiles instead of classical chart representations.

In one trading session, the time is not separated by the position of elements, but only with a typographical separation for different trading periods. These are called TPOs and one TPO usually represent a 30-minute period.

The principle of TPO can be represented in this picture.

On the left picture is a typical 30-minute timeframe chart. Individual candles are replaced by letters of the English alphabet, in the above example – starting with a letter D (picture in the middle). 

In the first period, Letter O highlights the opening price, and symbol # represents the price close in the last period.

At the end of each session, all the letters will line up how there is no space left between them.

This way, the Market Profile (last picture on the right) is finished.

From the TPO profile, we calculate POC (point of control) – line with the most amount of numbers and VA (value area) – 70% of letters around POC.

Market profile is not a trading strategy. It is only the way how to organize data.

And this is how I use it. I have read the CBOT Market profile guide, Daltons books, and seen some of his webinars, but I am not a market profile trader.

I have a market profile chart always open because it gives me instant information on where the value is, where we are trading and what anomalies we have at the chart.

I also believe that volume is more significant than time; I have all my settings based on volume instead of time.

To not make this guide an extra 20 pages longs when you buy a 400-page book where you will learn more about market profile from someone much more educated in the matter than me. 

I will quickly explain the market profile glossary and point out things I look at daily.

Initial Balance – First two TPOs (first two 30-minute candles) of a day. Every market has a different initial balance. 

CME products differ from different indices and commodities. ES for example opens at 9:30 EST.

Eurex products at 6 AM UTC.

Crypto at midnight UTC,

Forex can have pretty much anytime, such as 1st hours after London open, New York open, or whole Asian range.

I use initial balance as basic S/R lines; generally, you want to be long above and short below IB to stick to the day’s trend. If the price is too far from IB, you can play a mean reversion to it. Initial Balance, for me, is a factor of confluence, not a standalone entry decision.

 

Day Types – Normal Day, Neutral Day, Double Distribution Day, Trending Day.

There are even more of them; should you learn about all of them? Yes. 

Do I use them in my trading? No.

Trending and double distribution days are trend days, with trending day having the highest market conviction. You will often see one-time framing when the TPO doesn’t trade below the previous TPO’s low. In other words, the 30-minute candle doesn’t trade below the low of the previous 30-min candle in an uptrend. The opposite is true for the downtrend.

Double distribution has a lower conviction with a single print in the middle and two single distributions.

All the other days for me signal range days and balance, which built up for a bigger move in the future.

Opening Day Types – Open Drive, Open-test-drive, Open-rejection-reverse, open auction.

I don’t pay attention to these at all. They hold value when you can recognize them early on, but in my opinion, you don’t need to.

Poor Highs / Poor lows – Two or more TPOs at the same price point at top or bottom of the profile.

On a 30-minute chart, these are the double tops and bottoms. These signal us the unfinished auction, which is often revisited to complete the auction.

I use these as both targets and fading points.

Excess – Excess generally indicates the end of the auction and the start of another. 

Excess is seen in the extremes of the profile with a strong pullback. 

On the candlestick chart, you see this as a candle with a long wick.

As you can see, on this day market was trading lower until the U period, where it made excess to the low and auction reversed.

Because a candle signals excess with a long wick and rejection, I prefer to watch a 30-minute candlestick chart and footprint to see if anyone is trapped in the wick.

Because of that, I don’t pay too much attention to excess on the MP chart.

Single Prints – these are the same as excess but in the middle of the profile leaving Low Volume Nodes.

These show that the market might have been too quick to move in one direction. 

They often leave liquidity gaps, which tend to get filled, and because of them, I use these single prints as both target and entries.

Volume Profile

There are two ways to display an auction, with volume and price.

Which one is better? It’s up to you to decide, but my personal favorite is a Volume profile for showing Value Area and Point of control.

Value Area (VA) – 68% of the highest traded volume in the volume profile

Point of Control (POC) – Level of the most traded Volume. Naked Points of control bring the most significant as they are untested.

On the right side, you can see a volume profile representing one trading day; besides Value Area High, Value Area Low, and Point of Control, we also recognize the High volume nodes and Low Volume nodes.

High volume nodes (HVN) – areas where price found acceptance in the past, so they should act as support and resistance areas in the future; we want to use edges of these HVNs as our entries and exits.

Low volume nodes (LVN) – areas where price moved quickly through. Because of that, we can find liquidity gaps there.

Filling these LVNs brings us at the edges of High volume nodes where we want to execute trades, as I already mentioned.

To recap this, when the market is in HVN, we expect more of a range-bound environment with price bouncing from the edges.

When the market enters into the LVN, we can look for more trending environments.

Composite Profiles

Composite Profiles are all profiles that consist of more than one trading day. It can be days, weeks, or years.

Take a look at Dax Futures’ composite profile from the start of a drop in late February till bottom made in late March, the composite profile on marking the leg down and we can use it to predict future support and resistance levels.

We can use our auction market theory knowledge on HVNs and LVNs to find future support and resistance levels.

Another popular way of making a composite profile is by putting together multiple days with an overlapping value like this example with ten days of range.

This one we will revisit in the Trading Strategy chapter but here are some easy rules I follow when I decide if to merge profiles or not.

  1. If POC shift outside of the Value Area of the prior day I don’t shift profiles
  2. Trend days that break straight through the value without building new value does not violate the structure.
  3. More recent composite profiles are more important
  4. The more day the composite profile is built from, the more important it is.

As you can see here are 7 trading days.

Which ones we can merge?

Day one and day two are a no-brainer as they have overlapping POCs.

The third day moved completely lower so there is no merge there.

Forth day can be merged with the third, although it was a big trend day, POC and main value overlaps the third day.

The rest of the profiles are not a merge.

When the market is trading around these composite levels we can put more weight on them compared to single-day profiles.

VWAP

Although the Auction market theory or order flow did not yet get hyped up by YouTubers and different trading gurus, VWAP indeed did.

Being advertised as the next trading secret, VWAP is nothing more a moving average, but instead of being based on time, it’s based on volume and price.

Although having a bad reputation, it is a pretty good indicator to use.

I use the daily VWAP for each session.

If you have a problem with always trying to catch tops or bottoms in the market, try to implement a rule where you will only long above VWAP and short below VWAP.

Because VWAP attracts a large volume, I only recommend you fading touches of VWAP when price hits the VWAP with very sharp manner, in a lot of cases price will be more consolidating around the VWAP (same as price consolidate around the POC.)

VWAP 1 std. dev. bands can be viewed as developing value for the day.

The 100% same Auction market theory rules apply for VWAP bands.

When market accepts inside the bands, we can expect rotation to the opposite side.

If market accepts outside the bands we can use them as dynamic S/R for trend following.

Just bear in mind that these VWAP bands are anything but precise.

Thats why I use them just an guide rather than placing orders on them, for that I always use price action patterns.

VWAP is frequently watched by a lot of traders and that is the reason you should be watching it as well, don’t trade it blindly, use the patterns and overall context when trading around VWAP.

The FTR which will be in this case break and retest works best.

As you can see on the trading example market is staying below vwap through the whole session. 

This can save us from trying to catch a falling knife and focus rather on shorting. 

Later in the session market got accepted inside the st.dev band where we got the opportunity to trade it back to vwap.

Same as you can use daily VWAP with std. dev. bands, you can plot Weekly, Monthly, Quarterly and Yearly vwap with bands to your HTF charts.

The chart on the left is the 30-minute chart with weekly bands and the chart on the right is a 120min chart with monthly bands.

As you can see both VWAPs and their standard deviations acted as levels of support and resistance.

I have these plotted on my chart but I do not rely too heavily on them, they only act as the navigation to see if we are trending (the market is above/below the bands) or we are rotational (market moving from one extreme to another inside the bands) for given week and month.

 

Everything Delta

There are two types of orderflow. Active (market orders) and Passive (limit orders).

The Active Orderflow is usually called the aggressor as they don’t want to wait so they execute their orders at the bid or the offer.

Market orders are typically used by smaller players because they don’t care about the spread that much. 

If you are for example open 1 lot on German Bund $FGBL and get in at the market, the one tick you pay in the spread is worth 10€.

Trading only that 1 lot, 10€ spread is something you can live with, but if you want to execute 100 or 500 lots, you won’t be so happy to pay few thousands of Euros just in the spread.

Once you hit market order, you are taking liquidity from the market, during high impact news, for example, markets tend to get driven by the market orders, that means high volumes and low liquidity, cause high volatility.

Those traders that use market orders to get in the market are usually the weaker hand as they are less patient ones.

Passive order flow is the advertisement in the book, the resting bids and offers.

Large players typically use them, and they determine the market structure.

More often than not, they are a heavier hand in the market, making the liquidity. 

In a healthy trending market, you want to market and limit orders that come in hand, but they do not agree quite often, which is where you can find an edge in the market.

Cumulative Volume Delta shows us a running total of finalized executions at the bid and ask.

If the cumulative market volume delta is rising, buyers are more willing to trade into the offer than sellers hitting the bid. And vice versa,

CVD is not the leading price but shows us where we can find out absorption areas, which will present as divergences in the chart.

Another is the delta attached to the daily volume profile.

This shows us price levels where large buying and selling occurred. 

As you can see in the example, we had two areas with very high selling pressure displayed with long red delta bars during the down day.

In these areas, there are also FTR levels we can you can use as entry levels.

Because there was high selling pressure in the past, there are likely to be stacked orders, which will add more selling pressure on a retest.

Not only that, but also the opposite can be adding a confluence as well.

As you can notice, we have a big selling delta right at the VWAP, where the market formed over/under setup.

Because someone sold significant size there, he/she is currently sitting underwater and will gladly use the opportunity to get out for break-even or small loss.

This will add fuel to the upside as the sell order turned the market buys they exit.

The point of watching delta on the y-axis of the volume profile is the anomalies of high buying/selling.

Footprint Chart

The footprint chart offers us a look inside the candle with volume traded at exact prices.

You can watch a footprint chart on the monthly chart down to the tick chart.

Thanks to the footprint chart, we can see where sellers and buyers are interested and where they are trapped.

You can see sellers hitting the bid on the left side and buyers lifting the offer on the right side on a footprint chart.

Because I will not waste ten pages to show you everything about footprint here, I published a more comprehensive guide on my blog; if you haven’t read it, go check it out.

Here I am going to talk about the strategies I am using.

 

Buy/Sell Imbalance – Happens when buyers overcome sellers by more than 400% (due to my settings). Once we see the imbalance in our footprint chart, we can say that buyers/sellers are dominant in the market and move will likely to continue. You will see a lot of imbalance at the footprint chart. Thus, I only care about the situation where you have three imbalance above/below each other. These are called stacked imbalances.

During the breakouts, I use this to see how genuine the breakout was. If the breakout candle had high delta and stacked imbalances, price is likely to react on the retest where I enter at FTR.

Volume Imbalance – I use this one with SFP. It happens once you have a pin bar looking candle with a long wick, and most of the volume was traded in the wick. This means that there was an attempt to continue to the original direction, but the aggressive participants got absorbed. The ideal case is when you sell imbalances in the wick as well.

Finished/Unfinished Auction – These happen at tops and bottoms of candles. The unfinished auction occurs when you have both buying and selling at the top/bottom of candles. This means that market has unfinished business there and is likely to revisit the area in the future.

I don’t use this blindly as there is often not a price action structure for me to enter, but this is a great correlation for the trading ideas.

Trading SFP/Sweeps with Footprint – much less disaster

If you were paying attention, this would be just a simple recap of what I just showed you.

When I trade the SFP, my number one confirmation tool is a footprint chart.

The SFP can happen at a reasonable level with the deep swing, but I rarely take it without confirmation if I don’t see anything on the footprint.

So what I want to see in the footprint chart when trading SFP?

Volume imbalance is a must. There must be high participation inside the wick compared to the body.

Besides that, the sell imbalance or finished auction are extra points.

If the footprint is clear, I enter the trade with stop loss 2-3 ticks below the low.

If I am unsure of the setup, I go to lower timeframes and look for Over/under or FTR.

 

Other Price Action setups with Orderflow and Volume confluence

Over/Under – I mostly use this one as a reversal setup. 

Because of that I look for rejection at key reference points which are HTF HVN edges and pocs, previous day Value Area, VWAP stdev bands and daily ATR extremes.

If we are going to imagine the over/under as the head and shoulder pattern, I want to see CVD divergence between left shoulder and head with high selling/buying into the head and then quickly bouncing back giving me the signal that shorts/longs are trapped.

Once you are starting to look for over/under setups you will see them everywhere, especially on lower time frame.

Because of that I am very picky with those I trade, they have to happen at the reference point and I need to see at least some orderflow confluence.

FTR – This is my trend setup.

I look at break and retest inside or outside the previous day value area, HTF HVN levels, and VWAP.

If I see that breakout was genuine, which is often shown with the amount of delta on breakout bars, I look to enter the pullback at the top/bottom of the FTR.

As my confluence, I again use the footprint chart, CVD, and volume delta profile to see if any trapped traders will exit their position on a pullback.

I don’t categorize V-Shape as a standalone pattern as it is more of a price behavior, and there is always and FTR or O/U to enter.

If you want not to risk a large %, be in a good starting position where you can actually see your account growing and have enough money to cover margins (for legacy futures trading), the $10.000 is a bare minimum to start with.

Of course, you start with this amount once you are 100% in what you are doing.

Trading Strategy

Welcome to the last part of the Trading Blueprint.

This chapter will show you how I do my top-down analysis, what levels are important for me to watch, and how I execute around them.

All the concepts were already explained in the Blueprint before, so I won’t go into many details.

Daily Timeframe analysis

Used for marking out HTF levels.

The daily timeframe is important to watch because it is watched pretty much by everyone.

Although I generally don’t care about candle closes on a lower timeframe, daily closes are important as they are watched by most traders.

But marking out levels on a daily timeframe is also a little bit problematic.

Making a jump from D1 to tick/low timeframe chart chart is too big of a gap; that why you should not be too dependant on your daily levels and biases.

You can have a bullish bias based on the daily timeframe since the price bounced off the important support level, but it can take a few days for the market to move in the desired direction.

That’s why I don’t try to be too crazy about the D1 timeframe; if I have a bias based on daily timeframe or we are around the key daily level, I keep that in the back of my head by I don’t want that bias to kill my ability to take opposite direction if there is an opportunity.

On the daily timeframe, I mark out:

  1. Daily Bias Bars – Engulfing, SFP, Inside Day
  2. Price action based levels
  3. Composite POCs
  4. Option Strikes

Daily Bias Bars – explained in the Price action chapter.

Price action based levels that are in line with composite HTF profile – This sounds like a lot but in fact, it is not. My Daily chart consists of only one indicator, which is a composite volume profile that shows data from all bars. Why I do this instead of just marking out daily S/R levels? Because looking at HVNs and LVNs gives me a context of the market environment we are currently in. This goes back to Auction Market Theory Dynamics I mentioned previously.

When marking your levels you don’t want to go too crazy with it, I only mark out large HVNs tops, bottoms and POCs where we can clearly see value built. These levels should line with price action based S/R, you can also move them little to match candles bodies/wicks but don’t move them too much of course. Here take a look at the daily BTC chart, HVN bottoms are marked at the green, tops are red and POCs are yellow.  You can see how they line up with daily S/R? So this way you not only get solid support and resistance areas but also you will be able to understand in what market environment we are in.

 

 

30-minute chart

30 minute and Market profile chart are where my trade ideas come from.

Here is one thing about the 30-minute chart, which many people do with time-based charts, and I think it doesn’t make much sense.

I use the 30-minute chart because it is the same as the TPO Market profile and also it fits my trading style, but I don’t put much value on the closes of 30-minute candles.

I know a lot of strategies are based on the closing of candles “if price close above this level on this timeframe, I go long..”

In my eyes, candle periodicity is not so relevant as I can change the timeframe by adding a few minutes to fit my narrative.

The 30-minute timeframe for me is for spotting patterns and watching levels.

When it is time to execute, I care about an LTF/tick chart and how the market behaves there rather than how 30-minute candles are closing.

On a 30-minute chart, I look for:

First of all, you want to take a look at what is market doing based on the levels you marked on the D1 chart.  Once I have idea of what market is doing based on HTF context I look for possible price action setups:

  • FTR Setups
  • O/U Setups
  • Potential SFP (Stop run levels)

 

TPO chart

TPO Chart is great for building ideas using the Auction Market Theory rules I mentioned in the Blueprint several times.

I mark out all the Anomalies (Poor highs, poor lows and singles prints)

I mark out all nVPOCs that were not tested before.

Prior day profile is the most important.

I also put a composite profile once there is overlap of three and more days, this can be then used as small composite range.

So now that we have covered 30-min and TPO charts, here are the setups I usually take.

I know this seems like a lot, but after just a few days, you will find out that many of these PA and Market profile levels work very well together, and you use them to build confluence.

  1. 30 min SFP confirmed with Orderflow confirmation (CVD divergence, Footprint chart) and broken market structure on tick-chart
  2. Fading 30-min FTR,O/U either blindly placing limit if there is an untested tick FTR,O/U inside the structure or with price action confirmation
  3. Fading retest of untested nVPOC
  4. Break and retest outside previous day value area
  5. Acceptance inside previous day value area targeting the other side
  6. A retest of VWAP in line with 30-minute trend continuation
  7. Fading prior day high with orderflow confirmation (CVD divergence, Footprint chart)
  8. Taking a trade based on D1 levels (S/R, 30min structure during Daily Bias Candle, Acceptance in/out HTF node etc) – all these trades are mapped on 30 min chart before execution.

Execution Chart

This is a low timeframe tick chart I use.

Tick size is constantly changing, but here are some settings for different products. If you don’t want to use tick chart, you can use the 3/5 minute chart.

  • BTC (BitMEX data feed) 1000 tick chart
  • Bund 1000 tick chart
  • ES 3000 tick chart

The tick settings are constantly changing based on market volatility, so you will need to change them as well, general rule of thumb is you want to have approximately 100-150 bars displayed for one trading day.

I watch developing value for a day with a delta profile on the side of the execution timeframe. CVD, daily VWAP.

I use basic S/R, FTR, and O/U setups on the tick chart to enter the trades in terms of execution.

I usually prefer to wait for LTF confirmation during the trading day, but I don’t mind setting limit orders if there are clean levels.

On the lower timeframe chart, everything is about the location. You might be able to scalp different levels, but it is not what I do.

I always mark out different hypos based on 30 min chart/TPO chart and D1 context and I only trade them. I only look to capture 1-3 trades per day.

On the tick chart, I also watch for Footprint traps, finished/unfinished auctions and constantly look at CVD to spot absorption/exhaustion and divergence on a single bar.

I look at 30 minute and LTF deltas using the coloured candles from the suite.

The whole purpose of orderflow for me are two things: a) Spot trapped trades and b) spot strong breakouts.

I only care about orderflow when the market is trading at the location I want to trade, and when I am in trade to spot warning signals.

 

So here is the step by step breakdown of preparation for the trading day.

  1. Look at the daily timeframe, mark out daily support and resistance levels that line up with composite HVNs and POCs to understand the context of the market you trade.
  2. Move to 30 min chart. Look at the general direction of the market. Mark out untested FTRs (structures of broken support and resistance), O/Us, possible places where we can see stop runs – these are generally clean swing highs and lows, mostly from the prior day or week.
  3. Market profile chart. Mark out all anomalies, prior day value or composite value if you have several days of solid overlapping values, and untested nvPOCs.
  4. Execution Chart and Footprint Chart – I look at LTF patterns at my predetermined levels. Looking at PA and orderflow behaviour around the levels.

And that’s it.

There is no more to what I do, I know it doesn’t look very easy with many moving parts, but you will see how they are connected together.

A market profile is just a different graphical representation of a 30-minute chart. If you start execution at the strong levels that bring multiple confluence points from different chart together, you will see that ending up profitable for the day won’t be that hard.

For the intraday chart, the general rule is that trading along the daily trend is oftentimes much easier than trying to pick tops or bottoms.

If you are trying to fade moves, make sure you have a strong foundation for it – hitting the 30min/TPO/Daily level with clean PA pattern and orderflow confluence (CVD divergence, footprint trapped traders) with price accepting back into developing VWAP bands.

Following the trend is straightforward, I look for FTRs on the broken structure to jump in. I never chase breakouts; sometimes, the price won’t retrace to my entry, but I’m okay with that.

If you follow a trend, make sure you are not too late to the party (entering right before the HTF level where the turning point is likely to happen). The general rule is that price should be below/above VWAP bands and below/above the nearest VA (usually the prior day value). These trades usually take the path of least resistance until they meet the HTF level.

For any of my trade, my target is the First trouble area on the 30min/TPO chart; these are opposite sides of values, vwap bands, PA patterns and so on.

If I feel like trade can run, I trail my stop and am prepared to give back the profits.

I don’t scale in/out of trades because I think you don’t have a mathematical advantage. Once you start to take profits along the way, you will get really hit by a string of losses.

For intraday trading, I risk 1% per trade and when I hit 3 losses for a day I am closing everything and walking out.

This is not a trading psychology course and I am not a fan of trading psychology myself, but if you are trading lower timeframes, you can do a lot of damage if you keep revenge trading on LTF chart. This trading strategy provides 1-3 setups on most days, of course sometimes you get none sometimes you get more. My general sweet spot is if I take 2 trades a day, coming from this, if I hit a string of 3 losses in a row something is clearly wrong, so I walk away.
